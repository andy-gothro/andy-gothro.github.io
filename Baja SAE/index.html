Baja SAE

I was the Technical President of Gonzaga's Baja SAE team, and here is a collection of the work I've done:

My first major design project as a part of the Baja SAE team was as steering lead for my sophomore year. The preceding year, the front steering knuckle was one of the major points of failure during competition, and needed addressing.

During the competition, the front steering knuckle failed after a crash. The attachment point for the tie rod, which steers the wheel, was mounted in a cantilever setup, which when overstressed yielded due to both the tensile stress and torsion. The lower suspension mount also failed in shear due to an error in manufacturing making it thinner than designed.

During the event, the broken knucle was repaired by welding replacement mounting pieces onto the knuckle, which was successful, though difficult and cost a great deal of time during the 4 hour endurance race.

Addressing issues found at competition:

Following the competition, I took on a different system redesign, the rear suspension. I assumed the role of Co-president in January of my junior year to lead the technical side of the club. Of course things don't always go as planned, and we had some components break, which I led a redesign of afterward. The rear bearing carrier which attached the rear wheels to the driveshaft and suspension arms broke during the endurance event-twice, once on each side of the car.

During the 4 hour endurance race, we were forced to bring the car into the pits to perform major maintenance on the car twice to replace the rear bearing carriers on both sides. This cost us quite a bit of time during this fast-paced event.

Both times, the lower mount of the bearing carrier failed, leading to the rest of the suspension bendinf around itself as it came to a stop. Thankfully only the bearing carrier itself was permanently broken.

In this close-up of the failed piece it is clear that the part failed in bearing stress. This mount bore the entire load of the suspension and was the only place where any twisting forces were resisted, including the tractive force of the wheel.

In this image it is obvious that the area which failed was under-reinforced for the loads it was subjected to.

As a first concept at addressing the failures, I chose to explore a different suspension structure, instead of using a single small mount where the previous one failed, I chose to use a wider design to both lower the stress concentration and resist any torques.

I performed several different load analyses for this part, including the situation in shich the previous ones failed and other worst-case scenarios.

In addition to making a more durable design, I incorporated more adjustment into the attachment of the pieces, using ball-joints to adjust the alignment of the wheel.

Through the design iteration process, I opted to mimic the design of the front suspension for the new rear bearing carriers. This design is made of two bolted parts, to reduce machining complexity.

The new design of the rear bearing carrier allowed for the simplification of the rear suspension, making it function extremely similarly to the successful design of the front supension on the previous car.

Drivetrain work:

As we moved to a four-wheel-drive system, I led the design process and implementation of our own drivetrain components for the first time. I created an initial version of a differential which we would use to drive the front wheels:

Because of the interdependencies of the whole car, the design process had to be done in parallel through subassemblies. This is a render of everything which I worked on: rear drivetrain, rear suspension, ergonomics, and frame.

Because the team had never built a custom gearbox, I was forced to start my design from scratch. By researching other Baja teams' gearboxes, I decided that mine would benefit greatly from making it as mechanically simple as possible. I opted to separate each of the major components of the gearbox into 3 parts: the main reduction stage (using a chain), the rear bevel gearbox, and the shifting mechanism. Early in the design process I decided to use a sprocket and chain system as the main reduction to build in some adjustability by allowing for different spockets-this was invaluable as issues arised.

Though we were unable to attend competition due to the CoViD 19 pandemic, the car was completed and was successfully driven. Despite many unforseen issues and hiccups, I can confidently say that this was a great accomplishment and that the thousands of hours of work I put in were not wasted. Just on the drivetrain alone I worked to design a system of 50+ machined parts that work just as intended, with all tolerances, fit specifications, and seals working to accomplish a huge first for the club.

Components which I designed but were not realized

Though I did not design the steering rack which was used on the car, this refined version of the one used on the previous car was used as the basis for the final version.

I designed a custom differential early on in the design process, but we ultimately chose an off-the shelf honda atv differential for our front drivetrain.

The differential used off-the shelf gears, but all of the mounting and housing components were intended to be machined from billet pieces. At the time we did not have a sponsor with casting capabilities, but if actually chosen for the final design, the housing would have benefited greatly from casting and post machining.
