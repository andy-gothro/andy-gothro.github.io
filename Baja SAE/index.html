Addressing new issues found at competition:

Following the competition, I took on a different system redesign, the rear suspension. Of course things don't always go as planned, and we had some components break, which I led a redesign of afterward. The rear bearing carrier which attached the rear wheels to the driveshaft and suspension arms broke during the endurance event-twice, once on each side of the car.

During the 4 hour endurance race, we were forced to bring the car into the pits to perform major maintenance on the car twice to replace the rear bearing carriers on both sides. This cost us quite a bit of time during this fast-paced event.

Both times, the lower mount of the bearing carrier failed, leading to the rest of the suspension bendinf around itself as it came to a stop. Thankfully only the bearing carrier itself was permanently broken.

In this close-up of the failed piece it is clear that the part failed in bearing stress. This mount bore the entire load of the suspension and was the only place where any twisting forces were resisted, including the tractive force of the wheel.

In this image it is obvious that the area which failed was under-reinforced for the loads it was subjected to.

As a first concept at addressing the failures, I chose to explore a different suspension structure, instead of using a single small mount where the previous one failed, I chose to use a wider design to both lower the stress concentration and resist any torques.

I performed several different load analyses for this part, including the situation in shich the previous ones failed and other worst-case scenarios.

In addition to making a more durable design, I incorporated more adjustment into the attachment of the pieces, using ball-joints to adjust the alignment of the wheel.
